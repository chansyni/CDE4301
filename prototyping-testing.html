Prototyping and Testing

4.1. Initial Prototype

The grasper mechanism consists of curved arms designed to enclose the intestine (Figure 23). The arms are held apart by a spring at the apex; when the power supply is activated, electromagnets at the base of the arms generate an attractive force, causing the arms to close towards each other.

The initial prototype demonstrates the basic functionality of the mechanism (Figure 24). Although electromagnets are not yet incorporated into this prototype, a small force is used to simulate the attractive force. When the force is applied, the grasper remains closed; when the force is removed, the spring maintains the arms in an open position, effectively simulating the desired behaviour (Video 2).

This prototype is a low-fidelity model, enlarged for conceptual testing. The feasibility of implementing this mechanism at a smaller scale will need to be validated in future tests.

4.2. Prototype Parameters

Prior to advancing to the next prototype iteration, several critical parameters must be experimentally determined.

A primary consideration is identifying the optimal length for the grasper arms. Considering the human intestine’s approximate diameter of 2.5 cm, prototypes with varying arm lengths, ranging from 2.3 to 2.8 cm, will be tested. Each prototype will undergo usability testing on a 2.5 cm diameter tube to assess which length provides the most effective grip.

Additionally, key electrical parameters—including the current, coil count, and electromagnet area—must be specified to ensure that the magnetic force generated will be sufficient to overcome the spring force and successfully close the grasper. 4.3. Future Prototyping and Testing Plan

The prototyping and testing plans for each iteration are structured as follows:

  1. CAD Design and 3D Printing: Prototype parts are designed using CAD software and 3D printed to create the initial components for each iteration.

  2. Prototype Assembly: The 3D-printed parts are assembled to create a functional model ready for testing.

  3. Internal Testing for Workability: A multi-stage internal testing process is employed, moving from low to high fidelity. Each stage must demonstrate functionality before advancing:
    • Plastic Bag GI Model: The prototype is first tested on a simple plastic bag model simulating the GI tract. This allows for basic functional verification in a controlled setup.

    • Pig Stomach and Intestines (From Butcher): Following success on the plastic model, testing is conducted on real tissue sourced from a butcher, providing insights into performance on biological material.

    • Live Animal Testing (Pig): If available, live animal testing on a pig is conducted to observe the prototype in a dynamic, more realistic environment.

  4. User Testing with Bariatric Surgeons: After internal testing, the prototype is evaluated by bariatric surgeons to gather user feedback, assess clinical usability, and identify potential improvements.

  5. Analysis and Iterative Improvement: Testing results from each stage are analysed to determine necessary refinements. Improvements are then incorporated into the next iteration to enhance performance, reliability, and user satisfaction.

This iterative approach ensures progressive refinement, with each cycle bringing the prototype closer to clinical readiness and market viability.
