3.2. Initial Concept Generation

3.2.1. Endoscopic Surgical Stapler

The Endoscopic Surgical Stapler (ESS) (Figure 8) is a two-pronged device designed to staple and cut tissue placed between its prongs upon activation. The ESS is inserted into the patientâ€™s stomach trans-orally. The part of the stomach to be removed is involuted into the stapler between its prongs (Figure 9), where it is stapled to reduce gastric size, while the excess tissue is cut away. This process mimics SG by removing a portion of the stomach to reduce capacity.

3.2.2. Intestinal Sleeve

The Intestinal Sleeve (IS) (Figure 10) covers the proximal 200cm of the small intestine to prevent it from coming into contact with ingested material. This approach mimics the effect of a bypass, but rather than rerouting the ingested material, a physical or chemical barrier reduces digestion and nutrient absorption.

3.2.3. Dual Endoscopy

The Dual Endoscopy (DE) (Figure 11) method utilises 2 endoscopes during the procedure. The first endoscope is passed through the pylorus into the small intestine, and traverses 150-200cm of its length. The second endoscope exits the stomach through a hole made in the stomach wall into the peritoneum, where it locates the first endoscope. It then latches onto the small intestine at the same point and brings it up towards the stomach. Finally, an anastomosis is created between the stomach and small intestine, resulting in an anatomical configuration similar to that of the laparoscopic OAGB (Figure 12). The pylorus is blocked with another stent to minimise ingested material entering the original digestive pathway.

3.4. Concept Refinement

3.4.1. Functional Requirements

There are 4 key functional requirements for the procedure to be successful:

  1. Visualisation / Navigation: Enables the surgeon to monitor internal structures and guide instruments accurately.

  2. Intestinal Length Measurement: Ensures accurate measurement of 150-200cm segment of small intestine designated for bypass.

  3. Intestinal Manipulation: Lifts the intestine to align with the stomach in preparation for anastomosis.

  4. Anastomosis: Establishes an alternative pathway for ingested material, bypassing sections of the small intestine to decrease digestion and nutrient absorption.

3.4.2. Morphological Chart

Several solution principles were generated for each functional requirement, as outlined in the morphological chart (Figure 13). A solution principle was selected for each functional requirement, combined to form the overall concept solution.

Navigation / Visualisation
An endoscope with a camera attached at the tip traverses the GI tract, providing the surgeon with real-time internal imaging. Figure 14 illustrates this concept on a plastic model of the GI tract, while Figures 15 and 16 display images captured by the endoscope in the plastic model and the actual system respectively.

Intestinal Length Measurement
Markings on the endoscope is a straightforward way for intestinal length measurement. Upon reaching the pylorus, an external mark is made on the endoscope, followed by another mark 200cm further along (Figure 17). Once the endoscope is advanced to the 2nd marking, a 200cm segment of the intestine would have been traversed (Figure 18). However, current endoscopes range only from 25-200cm in length, which is insufficient for this purpose.

Intestinal Manipulation
A grasper grabs onto the intestine so that it can be elevated towards the stomach. However, a key limitation of current endoscopic graspers is their size (Figure 19), constrained to fit through a 5mm endoscopic channel (Figure 20). This increases the risk of unintentional intestinal perforations due to an insufficient grasping force to securely hold tissue.

An Axios stent (Figure 21) consists of 2 large flanges at opposite ends, which expand upon deployment (Video 1). These flanges have a wider radius than tissue openings, effectively securing tissue layers together. The stent also features a wide internal diameter, permitting the passage of ingested material, hence facilitating a successful anastomosis between the stomach and intestine (Figure 22).
